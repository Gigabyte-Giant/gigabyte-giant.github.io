About Me

Howdy, my name is Brynden, better known by my alias "Gigabyte Giant". I'm a teenager with a passion for pretty much anything related to technology, including (but not limited to) software and web development.

My journey into the land of technology started in the middle/late part of 2007, which is when I started creating "webpages" using Microsoft Word. After a few months of creating webpages using Microsoft Word, I was introduced to HTML and CSS, which I spent the next few months working with.

Some time after I started using HTML and CSS, my Dad introduced me to the fascinating world of Visual Studio, and with that, Visual Basic and C#. I don't remember doing much with Visual Basic, but I do remember spending a fair bit of time writing applications using Microsoft's "Small Basic" programming language.

At some point in time, I shifted my focus to C#.NET, with which I created a couple different applications, one of which was a web browser (which was heavily reliant on Microsoft's "WebView" control). The other application of interest, which was built in part with my Dad, was a note-taking application called "NoteTaker".

"NoteTaker" had two versions, the regular version, which contained not much more than a text editor and some buttons. The second version (which was called "NoteTaker Admin" for some reason that is unknown to me today) contained a text editor and buttons, along with a built-in web browser (again, nothing more than Microsoft's "WebView" control).

At some point during the lifetime of the "NoteTaker" project, my Dad introduced me to the concept of version control, through means of Mercurial and KilnHg (now called "FogBugz Dev Hub"). Without my "NoteTaker" project, I highly doubt I'd be as interested in programming as I am today.

Some time after I'd stopped working on the "NoteTaker" project, I purchased a copy of the voxel-based game "Minecraft". As many did, I spent countless hours playing Minecraft. Eventually however, I got bored, because there was nothing new for me to learn about the game. At that point, I decided to try my hand at writing server plugins and client mods. As some will know, this involves Java. I spent a year or so creating mods for Minecraft, but eventually ran out of ideas.

At some point in time (late-2012 or early-2013 I think), Khan Academy, which I'd used as part of my homeschooling, introduced a computer science curriculum. Since I was still fairly interested in programming, and because I was already a member of Khan Academy, I decided to try it out. During the early days of Khan Academy's computer science curriculum, they had a single course which taught the basics of JavaScript, with the addition of the Processing.js graphics library.

I spent a lot of time with Khan Academy's CS curriculum, and created a lot of programs that, at that point in time, seemed cool. My adventure with the Khan Academy CS curriculum, and Khan Academy as a whole, picked up quite a bit of steam in December of 2014, when I was approached about becoming a discussion moderator (referred to as a "Khan Academy Guardian"). I eventually found out that wasn't going to pan out, because of my age.

To make a [very] long story fairly short, I spent most of my time in 2015, contributing to Khan Academy's CS curriculum, by answering questions asked by other students. I also spent a lot of time contributing to one of Khan Academy's open-source projects, which was created for use with Khan Academy's CS curriculum. The project is called the "live-editor", and as the name might suggest, it's a browser-based code editor that contains a semi-live view of your code's output.

In 2015, while I was contributing to Khan Academy's live-editor project, I formed a team, and started a new project, designed to help Khan Academy employees judge the CS contests that they held. We, the team behind the project, spent a long time working together to build the web-based application. Our work paid off, as the Khan Academy team used the tool, and our GitHub repository was even starred by John Resig.

I spent a long time working on both the live-editor, and the contest judging application. My work was apparently recognized, as I was actually approached about becoming a contractor for Khan Academy, to work on their CS challenge framework, and/or their live-editor project. In the late part of 2015, after a lot of work, and a lot more waiting, I was informed that, because of my age, I would not be able to join Khan Academy's team as a contractor. As one could imagine, I was extremely disappointed.

Even though I didn't get a job after all my work, I learned a ton. I gained a lot of real-world experience, and I got to work closely with some really cool people, including those whose work history is extremely impressive (i.e. people that worked at Google).

In late-December of 2015, myself and the rest of the team behind the previously mentioned contest judging application, decided to abandon the project. I also made the decision that I was going to step away from Khan Academy, and work towards a job elsewhere.

If you move programming aside, you'll find that I'm also interested in endurance racing. My interest in the sport started in September of 2015, when I got the opportunity to head to Circuit of the Americas in Austin, Texas for the Lone Star Le Mans weekend, which turned out to be an absolutely amazing experience.

I also enjoy spending time with my dog, who I decided to name "Hex" (as in hexadecimal; yes, I'm a geek). Hex is a mix between a Labrador and a Border Collie. As is the case with most programmers, he spends a good bit of his time chasing bugs. He also enjoys playing ball, running around, and sleeping.

Originally, I was writing the library for use in one of my (non-public) projects, and wasn't going to make it public, but then I changed my mind.

Exif Extractor

This is another native library, and as the name might suggest, it was built to extract Exif information from JPEG files.

In my opinion, this is one of my most awesome projects. It is also one of my more difficult projects, mainly because when I started it, I had no prior knowledge about the Exif file format.

It took me a little over a week before I ever actually had anything working. I wrote many hundreds of lines of code just to help myself comprehend the Exif file format. I also read the Exif specification from cover-to-cover countless different times.
